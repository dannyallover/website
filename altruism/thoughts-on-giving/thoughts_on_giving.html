Thoughts on Giving

Donation, for me, has been a simple task (and perhaps should be): you see someone in need and you give. However, popularized donation dogma, effective altruism, complexes this natural process.

Here is effective altruism in a nutshell, as I see it. Effective altruism has two parts: (1) the deduction of values that we should care about (i.e. which causes should we give our money to), and, within those selected values, (2) the deduction of charities that will optimize our dollar. Examples at the extremes can sometimes help us best understand, so let me provide one. On the first matter (which values should we care about), is it, for example, sensible to donate all your money to bird reservations when there are people in Africa dying from starvation? One might reasonably conclude no, although I think it’s more complex than that, and I will revisit this. On the second part, once the value is chosen (say, saving human lives is the ultimate value), the next question naturally follows: how can I optimize my dollar? It has been proven that some charities are 100 or 1000 times more effective than others, so this raises the question: which ones are the most effective?

Here are my feelings on effective altruism. On the first matter -- choosing the highest value --, I think it's ok to stray away from this. I think it’s ok for people to donate to areas that hit home. We are individual creatures, each with our own unique problems, so if we all donate to efforts fighting our unique problems, then perhaps the world will balance itself out. When donating, I don’t objectively seek out the highest value, if one even exists, or if one can even do so; but if one wishes to try, they of course can (and more power to them). I think both paths are good. On the second matter -- optimizing our donated dollar --, I believe this is pragmatic guidance. This isn't to say we should be paranoid of inefficient charities, nor should this give us an excuse to not donate to someone in need, but rather is a guiding principle to sensibly encourage us to allow our dollar to do the most good.

To demonstrate how strict adherence to effective altruism can unearth some sticky scenarios, I will provide some hypotheticals:
(1) Suppose one dollar can provide Z impact. Thus, if I donate X dollars today, I can have Z*X impact. However, if I invest my X dollars today, I can increase my X dollars to Y (i.e. Y > X), and then I can have Z*Y impact (more impact than before). Should I not choose to invest then, rather than donate immediately? If I do choose to invest, when should I cash out to donate, since if I keep my money in the investing pool it will always become more (over time) and consequently donate more?
(2) Suppose I choose my value to be the aiding of blind folks. The cost to train a guide dog is estimated to be $40k, while the cost of curing a blind person in a developing country if they have trachoma is estimated at 20 to 50 dollars. If one follows canonical effective altruism, it seems inefficient to donate to guide dog initatives. Should we then rid guide dog non-profits altogether?
(3) Let's say our ultimate value is to save human lives. I could donate $5 to the Malaria Foundation and save one human life (which is prodigious impact). However, suppose that the earth is at risk of being hit by a meteor (you can choose a similarly motivated scenario like a rogue AI wiping us out), and that there's an initiative that tracks and mitigates this threat. Suppose that the chance of the earth being hit by a meteor, that would wipe human life as we know it, is a tenth of a percent. Let's also say that this organization needs a funding of 1 million dollars. If you work through the math, you can show that in expectation donating to the second cause has greater return. Therefore, in general, should we donate only to causes that mitigate exestential threats (e.g. meteor mitigation, rogue AI mitigation, etc)?
These prior examples are practical thought experiments that show effective altruism is non-trivial. That isn't to say that effective altruism is marred but rather its guidance principles can unearth an opaque path, and so should be taken with a grain of salt.
